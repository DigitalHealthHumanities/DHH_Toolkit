Why does understanding workflow matter? In a lot of ways, the exercises we did over this lesson have been over detailed, asking for you to think about the minute ways we bounce between different programs and tools in our research.  

These moments are taken for granted when they are smooth. Microsoft Word can export a .pdf file with little to no issue. We can drag and drop an image edited on GIMP into our Google Doc. We can upload files and send them over our email platform. 

This ease of use is, in many ways, an illusion, produced by decades of iterative design and professional training. They make us think that technology is *easy*, which is a lovely delusion right until a program stops working in the way we expect. 

In advocating for {% include term.html term="workflow" %} development, we are asking you to consider all of those frictions upfront. Developing {% include term.html term="prototypes" %} and {% include term.html term="protocols" %} is a hedge against as many complications that may arise in a long-term research project. In developing and concretizing a workflow, you are trying to be kind to your future self. It is more work upfront, but it will save time in the months and years to come. 

The goal of this lesson has been to help give you frameworks to think through potential snags early in your research, when you do not have to make a sunk-cost evaluation about whether or not you need to scrap weeks of work to get things to work. Workflows are a way of visualizing how every program and process links together. Prototypes give an opportunity to test out your approach to research. And protocols help reduce time spent relearning skills after months away. **The purpose of all of these practices is to streamline the processes early in the phase of research, so you can focus on the research itself.** 

What we encourage you to do, as you work on your project is not to outline an exhaustive workflow at the start, but to be aware of the labor involved when adopting specific technologies to answer a research question. It is always more complicated than we think at the start, and we can address workflow issues more effectively if we can recognize them early.
